Be Critical of the Information You Receive

Ask yourself, how biased is it?

Anthony   March 11, 2019 • 5 min read

One of the hallmarks of being an adult is the ability to make decisions and handle the outcome of those decisions. Another is being able to recognize that you may not have all of the data or knowledge required to make an informed decision and need to find that by asking more knowledgeable people and doing research of your own.

It’s important to remember that when asking people to share their knowledge that they are just people like us: they have thoughts, feelings, motives, and biases too. These shape the advice they give and must be accounted for when considering whether to follow it.

(Sub)Conscious Bias

A bias is a predisposition or tendency to favour a certain thing over another. You may really enjoy eating fish and seafood so whenever you go to a new restaurant you are biased to choose any dishes featuring them and over anything else on the menu. There’s nothing really wrong with this bias; it doesn’t hurt anyone except you and your desire to try new things.

However, when a bias guides the decisions that can affect other people, that’s when it can get a little dicey. Biases can turn in prejudices (preconceived feelings about a certain group of people) and cause people to discriminate (act on those prejudices) against those groups of people based on feelings that are not rooted in fact.

Biases can be thought of as either conscious (actively thought; intentional) or subconscious (existing in your mind but not in your active thoughts, like a memory; unintentional). A conscious bias could be interviewing only older applicants for a senior leadership role (biased towards older applicants as a senior job needs experience which comes with age) while a subconscious one may be coincidentally interviewing older men over women from the qualified pool of applicants.

Everyone has biases of their own but this doesn’t necessarily make advice that they give malicious by nature. A medical doctor who also follows homeopathy may prescribe painkillers but also be biased and suggest some kind of herbal tea to address your pain.

Advice is an offering of opinion that can be influenced by someone’s bias. It’s meant to be taken into consideration when making a decision of your own.

Underlying Motivation

Motivation is a driving factor that causes people to think or act in certain ways. Forms of motivation can be internal (coming from inside a person’s mind like a bias) or external (material gain in the form of money, items, or services).

Money is probably the most effective motivating factor for the majority of people. It’s what motivates us to go to work everyday, choose a specific type of investment, and compels people to play the lottery.

Compensation (money paid for work done) generally comes in the form of a set salary (hourly or annually; sometimes with the potential of overtime pay) and commission-based. A commission is compensation paid when a specific goal or task is completed, usually tied to meeting a sales target (flat amount paid when met) or as a percentage of the total sale amount.

Salespeople in Disguise

Commission-based compensation dominates sales professions as it directly motivates the salesperson to sell more; more sales means more money in their pocket. Usually this isn’t a big problem for most customers as they can figure out who is working on commission and whether the salesperson is recommending something they need or something that will make them the most commission (furniture salesperson recommending an entire living room set when you came in for a couch). It becomes problematic when you aren’t aware if someone is working on commission (financial advisors) or the potential sale (paid for with your money) is very large (real estate transactions).

In Canada we have financial advisers and financial advisors (that’s not a typo; the difference is one letter). Advisers are licensed and have to act as a fiduciary (one who puts clients’ interest first) while advisors are not necessarily licensed and are more often salespeople employed by a financial institution to push the products that yield the institution more money through commissions or higher fees.

If an advisor has a selection of funds to recommend but some will net them a commission of a few thousand dollars and they are not required to act as a fiduciary, which funds do you think they are motivated to recommend to you as an investor?

Buying a house is easily the largest transaction in most people’s lives and many (if not all) are carried out with the help of a realtor. Realtors work on commission and are usually paid 2-2.5% of the final sale price so they are motivated to make sure the price is as high as possible. As someone selling a house, this probably seems great; more money coming to you. As a buyer though, you may be pressured by your realtor to offer a lot more than you’re comfortable with just to potentially secure the highest bid.

In Ontario, Canada (and probably other places too), the blind bidding process hides important information about other bids from the buyers so there’s an implicit trust that has to exist between them and their realtors. If someone has a vested interest (personal stake in the matter, usually with a possible financial gain) tied proportionally to the price of the sale, do you think they are motivated to advise you to buy a cheaper or more expensive property given the option?

TL;DR

When receiving advice or a recommendation, consider who is offering it and whether their biases and motivation are affecting it to the point where your best interests are no longer the main focal point.
